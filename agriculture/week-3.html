Crops Across Canada

Growing Food: Seeds to Harvest  ·  Grade 6–7

What we're doing today
This week we step back from our grow space and zoom out to the scale of the whole country. Canada is one of the world's top agricultural producers — but most Canadians don't know much about what we grow, where, or why. Today we explore the five major crop categories and connect them to food systems, sustainability, and our own growing work.
Canada by the numbers
Canada has approximately 65 million hectares of farmland — about 7% of the country's total land area. Agriculture contributes over $140 billion to the Canadian economy annually, and Canada is a top global exporter of canola, wheat, pulses, and flaxseed. The Prairie provinces (Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba) produce the majority of Canada's field crops.
The five major crop categories
🫘
Pulse Crops
Legumes
What they are
Pulses are the edible seeds of legume plants — they grow in pods and are harvested dry. They are unusually important because their roots host bacteria that fix nitrogen from the air into the soil, naturally fertilizing the ground without chemical inputs.
Examples & growing regions
LentilsChickpeasDry peasNavy beansBlack beansFava beans
Saskatchewan grows over 95% of Canada's lentils — making Canada the world's largest lentil exporter.
SGC connection: Pulse crops reduce the need for synthetic nitrogen fertilizer because of their root bacteria. Rotating pulses with cereals is a key sustainable farming strategy that improves soil health.
🌾
Cereal Crops
Grains
What they are
Cereals are grasses grown for their edible grain (seeds). They are among the oldest cultivated crops in human history and form the base of diets worldwide. In Canada, cereals dominate the Prairie landscape.
Examples & growing regions
Wheat (spring & winter)BarleyOatsRyeTriticale
Saskatchewan, Alberta, and Manitoba account for nearly all Canadian wheat production. Canada is the world's 5th-largest wheat exporter.
Think about it: The flour in your bread, the oats in your porridge, the malt in many beverages — all cereals. Trace the grains in your last meal back to a Canadian Prairie farm.
🐄
Forage Crops
Livestock Feed
What they are
Forage crops are grown primarily to feed livestock — as fresh pasture, dried hay, or fermented silage. They are the foundation of Canada's beef, dairy, and sheep industries. Many forage crops are perennials, meaning they regrow year after year without replanting.
Examples & growing regions
AlfalfaTimothy grassCloverBromegrassCorn silage
Grown in every province — concentrated in Alberta (beef country) and the Maritimes for dairy. New Brunswick has a strong forage sector supporting dairy farming.
Local connection: New Brunswick's dairy farms rely heavily on local forage crops. The hay fields you see driving through rural NB are growing feed for the cows that produce your milk.
🌻
Oilseed Crops
Oils & Fats
What they are
Oilseed crops are grown for the oil extracted from their seeds. The oil is used in cooking, food processing, biodiesel fuel, and industrial products. After the oil is extracted, the leftover meal is high in protein and used as animal feed.
Examples & growing regions
CanolaSoybeansSunflowerFlaxseedMustard seed
Canada produces about 20% of the world's canola — primarily in Saskatchewan and Alberta. The iconic yellow fields of the Prairies in summer are canola in bloom.
Did you know? Canola was developed by Canadian scientists at the University of Manitoba in the 1970s. Its name comes from "Canadian oil, low acid." It is now Canada's most valuable crop export.
🌽
Specialty Crops
High Value
What they are
Specialty crops don't fit neatly into the other categories. They are typically higher-value crops grown for specific markets — food, industrial uses, or emerging sectors. In Canada, corn, rice (wild rice), and hemp are all considered specialty crops with distinct growing regions and uses.
Three key examples
CornWild riceHempPotatoesVegetablesFruits & berries
🌽 Corn
Canada grows both field corn (used for animal feed, ethanol, corn syrup, and starch) and sweet corn (eaten fresh). Ontario is the leading producer — the warmer, humid climate of southwestern Ontario creates ideal conditions. Field corn requires a long frost-free season of 120–140 days, limiting production to southern regions.
🌾 Wild Rice (Manoomin)
Canada does not grow Asian paddy rice on a commercial scale due to climate, but wild rice (Zizania species) is a native aquatic grain grown in the lakes and rivers of Ontario, Manitoba, and Saskatchewan. It holds deep cultural significance for many First Nations peoples, especially Anishinaabe communities who have harvested it for thousands of years. Canada is one of the world's top wild rice producers.
🌿 Hemp
Industrial hemp (Cannabis sativa, low-THC varieties) has been legally grown in Canada since 1998 and is a fast-growing specialty crop. Hemp seeds are used in food products (hemp hearts, oil, protein powder), while the fibre is used in textiles, building materials, and bioplastics. Saskatchewan, Manitoba, and Alberta are the top producers. Hemp is considered an environmentally beneficial crop — it improves soil structure, requires little pesticide, and sequesters carbon.
Today's activity — 50-minute class breakdown
1
Gallery Walk — Crop Cards  10 min
Your teacher will post one card per crop category around the room. Walk to each one, read it, and record in your journal: the category name, two examples, and one thing that surprised you. You don't have to write full sentences — point-form notes are fine.
2
Read & Annotate — This Page  15 min
Work through the five crop categories above. As you read, circle or highlight (or note in your journal): one fact you didn't know, one crop that connects to something you eat regularly, and one thing that connects to sustainability.
The specialty crops section has three sub-sections — corn, wild rice, and hemp. Make sure you read all three.
3
Small Group Discussion  10 min
In groups of 3–4, discuss: "Which of these five crop categories do you think is most important for food security in Canada, and why? Could any of our classroom crops be classified in these categories?" Each group shares one key point with the class.
4
Map Activity — Where Does It Grow?  10 min
On a blank outline map of Canada (provided), label the primary growing region for each of the five crop categories using a different colour or symbol per category. Add a legend. Think about: why does each crop grow where it does? What climate or soil factors matter?
Consider temperature range, rainfall, growing season length, and soil type when explaining your choices.
5
Journal + Exit Ticket  5 min
Complete the journal prompts below and submit the exit ticket before leaving.
